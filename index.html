Q1

What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

When I first landed on Rich Brown's website, I immediately saw the loading page. The page had a teal background colour, with the name "Rich Brown" split into two lines, one at the top and one at the bottom, all flashing dynamically as the page loaded. Instead of the usual dull spinner or a plain "x%" indicator, this one had visually appealing colour and captivating animations that kept my eyes on the page. And at the centre of the page, the loading progress is shown as "x%", keeping me in the loop.

This page looks more interesting than usually loading pages, and It made waiting for the page to load actually kind of enjoyable. It's a small detail, but it made the whole experience engaging right from the start.

Q2

Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

After the page loaded, I spent 2 minutes into exploring the website:

  • There was a large video background on the home page which caught my eyes. I watched it for about 10 seconds.
  • As I scrolled down using my mouse, I noticed the background video resizing slightly, transitioning from full coverage of the whole background to a smaller size on the centre of the page.
  • Then I moved my mouse around, I discovered that the cursor transformed into a small eclipse, looking like a pen tool that traced my movements with a white line.
  • On the page there was a large "R" in the top left corner and a corresponding "B" in the bottom left. Hovering my mouse over these letters caused the cursor to transform into a larger eclipse, and the letters responded to my movements, moving along with my cursor.
  • As I scrolled down further, and to my surprise, the page content shifted horizontally, sliding from left to right instead of the traditional top-to-bottom movement, adding an unexpected but fun twist to the browsing experience.

Q3

What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

I found myself spending the most time in exploring Rich Brown's portfolio. As I scrolled through the home page using my mouse, I could see his collection of works. Each piece was showcased through captivating videos, and they started playing automatically as they came into view. The transition from one project to the next was fluid, with the content sliding horizontally from left to right, with the background colour changing along the way, it felt like I was exploring a gallery in real life.

When I clicked on one of those videos, it took me to the detailed page of that particular project presented in text, images and more videos. As I scrolled through the details, I noticed that the outer bounding box of the elements transformed from a rectangle to a parallelogram, providing an intuitive visual cue that the page was moving in motion.

All of these small, cute interactions have made my experience of checking out Rich Brown's works become engaging and not mundane.

Q6

How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

From my point of view, the essence of interaction design here is not just about making the website look aesthetically pleasing. Instead, it's about creating a meaningful and appropriate user experience that guides audiences through the content, encouraging exploration and learning. By doing so, interactive experience can communicate its primary goal.

This website achieves its goals by using a variety of mediums such as videos, images, and text to convey information about the designer's work and highlight his design skills. Moreover, it incorporates interactive elements like animations and cursor transformations to encourage audiences’ further exploration. For example, the mouse cursor transforms into a larger eclipse when hovering over buttons (i.e the big “R” / “B” letter on home page), serving as a cue for clickable elements, and clicking them will open other content (i.e clicking the big “B” letter opens the designer’s Bio).

Q10

What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

These references provide visual cues and user-friendly design elements to guide user interactions:

  • Plain Text: This is straightforward information available around the entire website, without requiring any extra interaction. It suggests that I should read this content at their own pace.
  • Animated Text: On the home page, the use of rolling caption animations on the designer’s roles and awards draws immediate attention, indicating that this content is important and should be read first. This visual element is used to prioritise user attention.
  • Text Buttons: These buttons usually look the same as plain text at the first glance as they have similar font style and size. But they become apparent as clickable elements upon hover as the mouse cursor transforms into a big eclipse. This provides interactive cues and suggests that I should explore further by clicking those elements.
  • Auto-play Videos: Videos are automatically played in a loop, with short duration and no player controls. This suggests a quick engagement and encourages me to watch the content without any pauses.
  • Clickable Auto-play Videos: Certain videos serve as entry points to detailed project pages, with the cursor transformation indicating that they are clickable elements. This prompts me to explore additional content related to specific projects by clicking it.
  • Images: Static images can supplement project details. Some of the images have zoom functionality that allows me to view a higher resolution version. The cursor transformation into a big eclipse suggests that I should click and explore more on that image.
  • External Links: When I click on some clickable elements, it will only external links to the designer’s social media platforms, or external website for more project details. But clickable elements for external links don't have a unique design - they will look the same as other text buttons here and the cursor becomes a big eclipse when mouse hovering. I think there might be a need to improve clarity in design.
  • Background Visual Elements: As I scroll through the designer's projects list, the background colour changes and the thumbnail videos resize, signalling the transition to the next project. These small visual changes on background provide a simple cue to help me navigate through projects smoothly and make the most out of their exploration.

Q12

What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The most frustrating part was navigating back to the previous page after the detailed page of a specific project. I expected a familiar "<-" button for navigation, but it was missing on the detailed page. Therefore,I had to rely on the browser's back button, which was inconvenient.

After days of exploration, I discovered that clicking the big "R" letter in the top left corner served as the back button. However, this unconventional implementation of the back button. Users typically associate the back function with the "<-" symbol but rarely associate “go back” with the letter “R”. Without adequate visual cues or intuitive indicators, it just increased the learning curve of the interaction, causing unnecessary complexity and confusion when navigating between pages.

Q13

What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

The most satisfying part of this webpage's interaction for me is the dynamic movement effect triggered by scrolling the mouse. Besides text, the page also has captivating images and videos. As I scroll, they would elegantly slide onto the screen from left to right.

What makes that part satisfying is that the outer bounding box of these elements (videos, images, text) transform from a rectangle to a parallelogram during scrolling, and transition back to their original shape once scrolling stops. This is an innovative use of visuals for me, making this webpage a pleasure to interact with. Additionally, unlike the conventional up-and-down motion, the scrolling direction is left-to-right when I move the scroll wheel on my mouse. This unique motion design is very fun and makes the whole interaction experience very enjoyable and memorable.
